Combinatorial Game Theory

Impartial Games

Nim game: we are given some piles of chips. Two players play alternately. On a player's turn, they pick a pile and remove at least 1 chip. The first player who cannot make a move loses.

Lemma 2.1: In instances of Nim with two piles of n,mn,mn,m chips, it is a winning game if and only if m≠nm\neq nm=n.

Nim is an example of an impartial game. Conditions required for an impartial game:

  1. There are 2 players, player I and player II.
  2. There are several positions, with a starting position.
  3. A player performs one of a set of allowable moves, which depends only on the current position, and not on the player whose turn it is. ("impartial") each possible move generates an option.
  4. The players move alternately
  5. There is complete information
  6. There are no chance moves
  7. The first player with no available move loses
  8. The rules guarantee that games end

Games that are not impartial:

  • Tic-tac-toe (violates 3 and 7)
  • Chess (violates 3, players can only move their own pieces)
  • Monopoly (violates 6)
  • Poker (violates 5)

Other impartial games:

  • Subtraction game: we have one pile of n chips. A valid move is taking away 1,2, or 3 chips. The first player who cannot move loses.
  • Rook game: we have an m×nm\times nm×n chess board, and a rook in position (i,j). A valid move is moving the rook any number of spaces left or up. The first player who cannot move loses.
  • Green hackenbush game: we have a graph and a floor. The graph is attached to the floor at some vertices. A move consists of removing an edge of the graph, and any part of the graph not connected to the floor is removed. The first player who cannot move loses.

A main result we will prove is that all impartial games are essentially like a Nim game.

Definition: a game HHH that is reachable from game GGG by a sequence of allowable moves is simpler than GGG.

Lemma 2.2: in any game GGG, either player I or player II has a winning strategy.

Proof: we prove by induction on the simplicity of GGG. If GGG has no allowable moves, then player I loses, so player II has a winning strategy. Assume GGG has allowable moves and the lemma holds for games simpler than GGG. Among all options of GGG, if player I has a winning strategy in one of them, then player I moves to that option and wins. Otherwise, player II has a winning strategy for all options. So regardless of player I's move, player II wins.□\square□

So every impartial game is either a winning game (Player I has a winning strategy) or a losing game (Player II has a winning strategy). We assume players play perfectly. If there is a winning move, then they will take it.

  • A winning game has at least one winning move
  • A losing game: all options are losing games.

Poker Nim: a variation of Nim. Poker nim consists of a regular Nim game plus a bag of BBB chips. We now allow regular Nim moves and adding B′≤BB'\le BB′≤B chips to one pile. Example: ∗3+∗4→∗53+∗4*3+*4\rightarrow *53+*4∗3+∗4→∗53+∗4.

How does this change the game of Nim? Nothing. Say we face a losing game, so any regular Nim move would lead to a loss. In poker nim, we now add some chips to one pile. The opposing player will simply remove the chips we placed, and nothing changed. When we say that a game is equivalent to a Nim game with one pile, it is actually a game of poker nim with one pile.

Strategic Games

Prisoner's dilemma (game show version): 2 players won 10,000. They each need to make a final decision: "share" or "steal"

  • If both pick "share", then they each win 5000 dollars
  • If one picks "steal" and the other picks "share", then the one who pick "steal" gets 10000 dollars, the other gets nothing
  • If both pick "steal", then they both get a small consolation prize with 10 dollars

Definition: A strategic game is defined by specifying the set N={1,…,n}N=\{1,\dots, n\}N={1,…,n} of players, and for each player i∈Ni\in Ni∈N, there is a set of possible strategies sis_isi​ to play, and a utility function ParseError: KaTeX parse error: Undefined control sequence: \time at position 22: …_1\times \dots \̲t̲i̲m̲e̲ ̲s_n \rightarrow….

Assumptions about strategic games:

  1. All players are rational and selfish (want to maximize their own utility).
  2. All players have knowledge of all game parameters
  3. All players move simultaneously.
  4. Player iii plays a strategy si∈Sis_i\in S_isi​∈Si​, this forms a strategy profile s=(s1,…,sn)∈S1×⋯×Sns=(s_1,\dots, s_n)\in S_1\times \dots \times S_ns=(s1​,…,sn​)∈S1​×⋯×Sn​. Player iii earns ui(s)u_i(s)ui​(s).

Given a strategic game, what are we looking for? One answer is we want to know how are the players expected to behave?

Prisoner's dilemma is an example of a strictly dominating strategy: regardless of how other players behave, this strategy gives the best utility over all other possible strategies. If a strictly dominating strategy exists, then we expect the players to play it.

Nash equilibrium (NE)

There are many games with "no" strictly dominating strategies. The steady states are called Nash equilibria: a strategy profile where no player is incentivized to change strategy.

Mixed Strategies: there are many games with no Nash equilibria. (e.g. Rock Paper Scissor).

Nash's Theorem: every strategy game with finite number of strategies has a Nash equilibrium. (could be mixed strategies).
